General risk profile for this combination

Moderate Risk — Action Advised

Australia and the Philippines have a tax treaty. This is one of the most common remote work arrangements in the Asia-Pacific region. The Philippines uses a 180-day threshold. Australian companies frequently engage Filipino workers as both employees and contractors. PE risk is low for contractor arrangements. For employee arrangements, payroll compliance in the Philippines requires attention as local employment law is detailed.

Income tax exposure for the worker

Once the worker has been physically present in Philippines for more than 180 days, they generally become a Philippines tax resident. This means Philippines gains the right to tax their worldwide income — including the salary paid by their Australia employer.

If a tax treaty exists between Australia and Philippines, double taxation can usually be avoided — but treaty relief must be actively claimed on the worker's tax return. It does not apply automatically.

Workers approaching the residency threshold should track their days carefully and speak to a cross-border tax professional before crossing it.

Permanent establishment risk for the employer

A Australia company with a remote employee in Philippines may be creating a taxable corporate presence — a permanent establishment — in Philippines, depending on the nature of the role and how long the arrangement has been in place.

  • Low risk roles — technical, administrative, or support staff with no local client contact and no contract signing authority
  • Higher risk roles — sales, executive, or client-facing staff who serve the Philippines market directly
  • Immediate risk — any employee with authority to sign contracts on behalf of the company, regardless of role or days present

Under OECD 2025 guidelines, working above 50% of time in Philippines combined with a commercially connected role significantly raises PE risk for the employer.

Payroll and compliance obligations

If the worker is an employee — not an independent contractor — the Australia employer may need to register for payroll in Philippines or use an Employer of Record service once the worker becomes a Philippines tax resident.

Running payroll entirely from Australia for a permanent Philippines-resident employee creates compliance risk for the employer. An Employer of Record service is often the fastest route to compliance without establishing a local entity.
