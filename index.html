    Understanding your results

    What does each risk level mean?

    Our tool checks three separate risk categories for your situation. Each one gets its own colour-coded rating based on the information you provide.

    Green — Low risk

    Your situation is unlikely to trigger tax obligations or compliance issues based on current rules. You should still monitor your days and keep your situation documented.

    Amber — Moderate risk

    There are factors in your situation that create real exposure. You do not need to panic, but you do need to take action. Speaking to a professional soon is strongly recommended.

    Red — High risk

    Your arrangement has serious legal or financial exposure that needs immediate attention. This does not mean you have done something wrong — but it does mean the current setup needs to change.

    Common situations

    See how it works for real scenarios

    Here are four common cross-border remote work situations and how the tool assesses them.

    Low Risk

    US employer · Nigeria contractor · 90 days · Technical role

    An independent contractor writing code for a US company from Nigeria, working there 90 days. No signing authority, no permanent arrangement. Result: minimal corporate tax exposure and no payroll withholding obligation.

    Moderate Risk

    UK employer · Portugal employee · 150 days · Technical role

    A developer employed by a UK company working permanently from Portugal for 150 days. A UK-Portugal tax treaty exists. Risk is moderate — approaching the 183-day threshold and a treaty claim needs to be filed to avoid double taxation.

    High Risk

    US employer · UK employee · 200 days · Executive with signing authority

    A senior executive employed by a US company, living in the UK for 200 days with authority to sign contracts. This triggers UK tax residency, creates a high PE risk for the employer, and requires urgent payroll restructuring.

    Moderate Risk

    Germany employer · Philippines employee · Permanent · Sales role

    A sales employee permanently based in the Philippines working for a German company. The sales function combined with permanent arrangement creates moderate PE risk even though a Germany-Philippines tax treaty exists.

    Frequently asked questions

    Questions people ask before using this tool

    The 183-day rule is the most common threshold countries use to determine tax residency — if you spend more than 183 days in a country in a calendar year, you generally become a tax resident there. However, several countries use different thresholds: the UK uses a complex ties-based test where even 46 days can trigger residency, the US uses a weighted three-year formula, and countries like Thailand (180 days), Malaysia (182 days), and Cyprus (60 days) have their own variations. Our tool applies the correct threshold for your specific country.
    Permanent establishment (PE) means your employer is considered to have a taxable business presence in your country — even if they have no office there. If a PE is triggered, your employer could owe corporate income tax in your country. This is a serious exposure for the company, not just for you. The biggest triggers are: having an employee with authority to sign contracts, having a sales or executive employee working there long-term, or having an employee who spends more than 50% of their working time there serving local clients.

Tax treaties can significantly reduce double taxation risk and our assessment takes this into account. However, treaty benefits are not automatic — they generally need to be actively claimed on your tax return. For less common country combinations, the tool applies general OECD principles and flags where you should verify treaty status.
    Yes, but your risk profile is very different. Independent contractors rarely create permanent establishment risk for the engaging company, and you handle your own tax obligations rather than triggering employer payroll requirements. However, you are still responsible for declaring and paying income tax in your country of residence. The tool explains what that means for your specific situation. One critical warning: if your arrangement looks like employment in practice — fixed hours, one client, employer control — tax authorities may reclassify you as an employee regardless of what the contract says.

    An Employer of Record (EOR) is a third-party company that legally employs workers on behalf of another company in a foreign country. If you are an employee working long-term in a country where your employer has no legal entity, an EOR is often the cleanest solution — it handles local payroll, tax registrations, and employment law compliance. Services like Deel, Remote.com, and Rippling offer this. Our tool recommends an EOR where it is relevant to your situation.
