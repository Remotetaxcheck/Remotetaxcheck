Tax Residency

The 183-day rule explained without the lawyer speak

Updated June 2026 · 6 min read · Informational only — not tax advice

Quick answer

The 183-day rule is the threshold most countries use to decide if you are a tax resident. Spend more than 183 days in a country in a given period and that country generally has the right to tax your income. But "183 days" is not universal — the UK uses a completely different test, the US uses a three-year formula, and countries like Cyprus trigger residency at just 60 days. Knowing your specific country's rule is the only thing that matters.

The 183-day rule is one of those things that sounds simple until you need to actually apply it to your life. Then it gets complicated fast. Different countries count days differently. Some use calendar years, some use rolling 12-month periods. Some have extra conditions on top of the day count. And two of the world's most important countries for remote workers — the UK and the US — do not even use the standard rule.

Here is everything you need to know, in plain language.

What the 183-day rule actually is

Tax residency determines which country has the right to tax your income. Most countries use physical presence as the main test — if you spend enough time somewhere, that country considers you a resident and taxes you accordingly.

183 days became the standard threshold because it represents roughly half a year. The logic is: if you spend more than half your year in a country, that country has a reasonable claim to be your main tax home.

Once you become a tax resident somewhere, that country typically taxes your worldwide income — not just income earned within its borders. This is why the threshold matters so much for remote workers.

How the days are counted

This is where people make expensive mistakes. Days are generally counted as any day you are physically present in the country — including the day you arrive and the day you leave in many jurisdictions. A single hour spent in a country on a transit stop can count as a full day in some places.

The counting period also varies:

  • Calendar year — January 1 to December 31. Most countries use this. Germany, France, Spain, Poland, Romania all count on a calendar year basis.
  • Rolling 12 months — any 12-month period, not just a calendar year. Portugal, Brazil, Colombia use this approach. It means you can trigger residency faster than you think if you arrive mid-year.
  • Tax year — some countries use their own fiscal year rather than the calendar year. The UK tax year runs April 6 to April 5.

The UK exception — why it is different

The UK does not use a simple 183-day rule. It uses what is called the Statutory Residence Test, which looks at a combination of days spent in the UK and your connections — called ties — to the UK.

The more ties you have, the fewer days it takes to become a UK resident. Ties include: having a UK home, having a spouse or minor children in the UK, doing substantive work in the UK, and having spent more than 90 days in the UK in either of the two previous tax years.

The critical numbers are:

  • Fewer than 16 days in the UK — automatically not resident, regardless of ties
  • 16 to 45 days — resident only if you have 4 ties
  • 46 to 90 days — resident if you have 3 or more ties
  • 91 to 120 days — resident if you have 2 or more ties
  • 121 to 182 days — resident if you have 1 or more ties
  • 183 or more days — automatically resident, regardless of ties

This means a person with strong UK connections — family, a home, recent work history there — can become a UK tax resident with as few as 46 days in the country. That is a dramatically lower threshold than most people expect.

Pro tip

If you have recently left the UK to work remotely abroad, count your UK ties carefully. Many people assume they only need to worry about days. If you still have a UK home available to you or your partner and children are in the UK, you may become a UK resident with far fewer days than you think.

The US formula — why it is complicated

The US uses what it calls the Substantial Presence Test, which looks at three years of data rather than just the current year. The formula is:

All days in the current year, plus one third of days in the previous year, plus one sixth of days in the year before that. If the total is 183 or more, you are considered a US tax resident for that year.

This means someone who has been spending significant time in the US over multiple years can trigger residency in a year when they spent fewer than 183 days there — because their prior years count against them at a fraction of their weight.

And separately from residency rules, US citizens and green card holders are taxed on worldwide income regardless of where they live. This is unique among major economies and means the 183-day rule is essentially irrelevant for US nationals — they owe US tax no matter where they are.

What actually gets triggered when you cross it

When you cross the residency threshold in a country, that country gains the right to tax your worldwide income for that tax year. What that means in practice:

  • You need to file a tax return in that country
  • You declare all your income — not just income from local sources
  • If you are also a resident of another country, you may be taxed by both — unless a tax treaty between the two countries prevents this
  • Your employer may need to adjust its payroll arrangements

How to track your days properly

The simplest method is a dedicated spreadsheet or notes app where you log every day you are in each country. Passport stamps help but are not always stamped for travel within the Schengen Area or across borders without checkpoints.

A better method is to use your phone's location history or banking transaction data as a secondary record. If HMRC or another tax authority ever questions your day count, having corroborating evidence from multiple sources is far stronger than a spreadsheet alone.

Track the day you arrive and the day you leave separately. In most countries both count as presence days.
