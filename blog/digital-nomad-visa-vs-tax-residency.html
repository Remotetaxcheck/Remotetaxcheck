Digital nomad visa vs tax residency — they are not the same thing and here is why it matters

Updated June 2026 · 7 min read · Informational only — not tax advice

Quick answer

A digital nomad visa gives you the legal right to live and work in a country. Tax residency determines which country can tax your income. These are two completely separate legal concepts governed by completely separate rules. Having a digital nomad visa does not make you a tax resident. Not having one does not stop you becoming one. You need to address both independently.

The digital nomad visa boom has been one of the most significant immigration developments of the last five years. Over 60 countries now offer some version of a visa specifically for remote workers — from Portugal and Spain to Thailand, Indonesia, and Costa Rica.

But with the marketing around these visas has come a widespread and expensive misconception: that getting a digital nomad visa somehow sorts out your tax situation. It does not. In some cases, it makes things more complicated.

Where the confusion comes from

The confusion is understandable. Governments market digital nomad visas as a complete solution for remote workers who want to live abroad legally. The visa gives you the right to be there. It deals with immigration law. And many of the countries offering these visas have simultaneously introduced tax incentives for the same category of people.

When a country launches a digital nomad visa program and a tax incentive at the same time, people naturally assume the two are connected. Sometimes they are — sometimes getting the visa automatically registers you in a particular tax regime. But often they are entirely separate systems operated by different government departments, and you need to engage with both independently.

What a digital nomad visa actually does

A digital nomad visa is an immigration document. It answers one question: are you legally allowed to be in this country and work remotely from here?

What it grants:

  • Legal right to reside in the country for the visa period
  • Legal right to work remotely for foreign employers or clients
  • In some countries, access to local banking, healthcare registration, or other resident services
  • A path to longer-term residency in some jurisdictions

What it does not do:

  • It does not determine your tax residency status
  • It does not exempt you from local income tax unless a specific exemption is attached to the visa program
  • It does not affect your tax obligations in your home country
  • It does not tell your employer anything about their compliance obligations

What tax residency actually does

Tax residency is determined by tax law, not immigration law. The two systems operate in parallel and do not automatically inform each other.

Tax residency determines which country has the right to tax your worldwide income. In most countries this is triggered by physical presence — typically 183 days, though the exact threshold varies significantly by country as we explain in our 183-day rule guide.

Critically, you can become a tax resident of a country without any visa at all — simply by spending enough days there. And you can hold a valid digital nomad visa without becoming a tax resident if you leave before crossing the residency threshold.

Digital nomad visa

  • Immigration document
  • Issued by immigration authority
  • Based on your income level and employer
  • Grants right to live and work remotely
  • Fixed duration — typically 1-2 years
  • You apply for it deliberately

Tax residency

  • Tax status
  • Determined by tax authority
  • Based on days present and connections
  • Grants country right to tax your income
  • Resets each tax year
  • Can happen to you without applying

How specific countries handle both

Portugal — IFICI (NHR 2.0)

Portugal's digital nomad visa and the IFICI tax regime are separate applications. The visa gets you legal residence. IFICI — available to qualifying professions including technology workers — provides a favourable 20% flat tax rate. You apply for each separately. Having the visa does not automatically put you in the IFICI regime, and you can qualify for IFICI without the specific nomad visa.

Spain — Beckham Law

Spain offers a digital nomad visa under its Startup Act alongside the so-called Beckham Law tax regime, which provides a flat 24% rate for qualifying individuals. Again these are two separate applications. The visa does not enrol you in the tax regime. You must apply for the special tax regime separately within six months of registering as a Spanish tax resident.

Thailand — LTR Visa

Thailand's Long-Term Resident visa for remote workers comes with specific tax exemptions on foreign-sourced income — but only for the LTR visa category specifically, and only if the income was earned outside Thailand. Standard tax residency rules still apply for Thailand-sourced income. The visa and the exemption are linked in this case, but the rules are narrow.

Indonesia — Second Home Visa

Indonesia's visa for remote workers does not come with any specific tax exemption. Standard Indonesian tax residency rules apply — if you spend 183 days or more in Indonesia in a 12-month period, you are an Indonesian tax resident. The visa makes your stay legal; it does not change your tax position.

Three situations and how they play out

Situation 1 — You have a nomad visa and stay under the residency threshold

You are legally in the country, working remotely. You leave before crossing 183 days. You are not a tax resident. Your income remains taxable in your home country only. The visa did its job — it made your stay legal. Tax: no change from home country rules.

Situation 2 — You have a nomad visa and stay past the residency threshold

You are legally in the country. You cross 183 days. You become a tax resident regardless of what your visa says. You now have tax filing obligations locally. If a tax treaty exists between your home country and this one, you need to claim relief to avoid double taxation. The visa made your stay legal — but did not change what the tax threshold does when you cross it.

Situation 3 — You have no nomad visa but stay past the residency threshold

You are illegally present (no valid visa), but you may still be a tax resident. Tax authorities do not require a visa as a condition of assessing you as a resident — they look at days present. You face both immigration and tax exposure simultaneously. This situation is more common than people think among short-term travellers who extend their stay.

Pro tip

Check whether the country you are targeting has a specific tax exemption attached to its nomad visa program — and if so, whether it applies automatically or requires a separate application. Thailand's LTR exemption, Portugal's IFICI, and Spain's Beckham Law all require separate applications with deadlines. Missing the window means paying standard rates even if you qualified.

What to do if you have or want a nomad visa

  • Research the tax rules for that country separately from the visa rules. They are different documents from different government departments.
  • Find out whether the country has a specific tax regime for nomad visa holders — and if so, what the application process and deadline is.
  • Track your days carefully from the moment you arrive. The residency threshold applies whether you planned to cross it or not.
  • Check your home country obligations — in most cases, leaving does not immediately end your home country tax residency. You need to formally exit the tax system in most countries.
  • Tell your employer — the visa makes your presence legal but it does not resolve your employer's payroll and PE obligations. Those need to be addressed separately.
  • Get advice for the first year — cross-border tax in the year you move is always the most complicated. A one-time consultation with a specialist pays for itself.
