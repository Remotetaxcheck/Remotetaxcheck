Contractor vs Employee

Contractor vs employee abroad — why the difference could cost your company thousands

Updated June 2026 · 9 min read · Informational only — not tax advice

Quick answer

Whether someone working for you abroad is classified as an employee or independent contractor changes virtually everything about your cross-border tax exposure. Contractors rarely create PE risk or payroll obligations for you. Employees almost always do if they stay long enough or have the wrong role. And if the authorities decide your contractor is really an employee — a process called misclassification — you face back taxes, penalties, and potential legal exposure in a foreign country.

When a company first lets someone work abroad, the question of employee versus contractor often feels like a formality. You have a contract. It says contractor. Surely that settles it.

It does not. And the gap between what a contract says and how a tax authority interprets the real working relationship is where most cross-border compliance problems begin.

Why the classification matters so much

Employee and contractor are not just different words for the same thing. They create entirely different legal relationships — different tax obligations, different social security requirements, different employment law protections, and critically different exposure for the company engaging the worker.

In a cross-border context, the classification determines three major things: whether the company has permanent establishment risk in the worker's country, whether the company has payroll registration obligations there, and who bears responsibility for local tax compliance.

Misclassification — the hidden danger

Misclassification happens when a worker is called a contractor in their contract but the reality of the working relationship looks like employment. Tax authorities in the UK, Germany, France, Australia and many other countries actively investigate this — and they look at the substance of the relationship, not the label on the contract.

If they decide your contractor is really an employee, the consequences hit the company hard. You may owe back payroll taxes for every month they worked. You may owe social security contributions you never paid. You face penalties for non-compliance. And in some countries the worker gains employment rights retroactively — things like severance entitlements, paid leave, and unfair dismissal protection.

Pro tip

The more your contractor looks like a dedicated employee in practice — fixed hours, single client, no ability to send a substitute, work directed by your company day to day — the higher your misclassification risk. It does not matter what the contract says if the reality contradicts it.

How tax authorities decide which you are

Different countries use slightly different tests, but most look at the same core factors.

  • Control — does the company control how and when the work is done, or just what is delivered? More control points toward employment.
  • Substitution — can the contractor send someone else to do the work? Genuine contractors can. Employees cannot.
  • Economic dependence — does the contractor work almost exclusively for one company? High economic dependence on a single client is an employment indicator in many jurisdictions.
  • Integration — is the worker integrated into the company's operations, systems, and team culture? Integration is an employment signal.
  • Financial risk — does the worker bear real financial risk — the possibility of making a loss, investing their own money, or being responsible for correcting their own mistakes? Contractors typically do. Employees typically do not.

What contractors still need to know

Even a genuinely independent contractor working abroad is not without obligations. The worker is still a tax resident somewhere and still responsible for declaring their income and paying tax in their country of residence. The engaging company may not have payroll obligations — but the individual absolutely has personal tax obligations.

Contractors also need to be aware that their income may be subject to VAT or local sales tax in some jurisdictions, particularly if they are providing services to businesses in the EU.

When to consider restructuring

If your working relationship with someone abroad has evolved to the point where misclassification risk is real — or where the person has been working as a contractor for years but the arrangement looks like employment — the honest answer is that the arrangement needs to be restructured.

Options include: formally employing the person through a local entity or Employer of Record service, genuinely restructuring the relationship to be more clearly contractor-like, or ending the arrangement entirely and replacing it with a properly documented engagement.

None of these is painless. But they are all better than a misclassification finding years down the line with accumulated back taxes and penalties.
