I moved to Portugal but my employer is still in London — do I owe tax twice?

Updated June 2026 · 8 min read · Informational only — not tax advice

Quick answer

Probably not — but only if you actively claim relief under the UK-Portugal tax treaty. The treaty exists to prevent exactly this situation, but it does not apply automatically. You need to file correctly in both countries and tell your employer what has changed. If you do nothing, you may end up taxed in both places.

You made the move. You are sitting in Lisbon or Porto with your laptop, doing the same job you always did, sending work to the same London office. But now you are wondering whether the Portuguese tax authority has any claim on your income — and whether HMRC still does too.

This is one of the most common situations we see, and the answer is more manageable than most people fear — but only if you handle it correctly.

What is actually happening with your tax

When you move to Portugal and live there for more than 183 days in a calendar year, you become a Portuguese tax resident. That is not a choice — it is what the law says happens. Portugal then has the right to tax your worldwide income, including the salary your London employer is paying you.

At the same time, your UK employer is likely still running your payroll through the UK system and deducting UK income tax through PAYE. So on paper, you have two countries each with a claim on the same income.

This is called double taxation risk. And it is exactly the situation the UK-Portugal tax treaty was designed to prevent.

What the UK-Portugal tax treaty does

The UK and Portugal have had a double taxation agreement in place since 1969, updated over the years. What it means in practice is this: you should not have to pay full income tax in both countries on the same earnings.

Under the treaty, your income is generally taxable in the country where you physically perform the work. Since you are physically working in Portugal, Portugal gets the primary right to tax your employment income once you are a resident there.

The UK can still tax you if you have UK-sourced income — but for most remote workers, the salary you earn while sitting in Lisbon is considered Portuguese-source income under the treaty rules.

Pro tip

The treaty does not apply automatically. You need to claim it. In the UK this means completing a form to notify HMRC of your non-resident status and potentially claiming a refund if UK tax has been over-withheld. In Portugal it means declaring your income and referencing the treaty on your Portuguese tax return. Get a cross-border tax advisor to handle the first year — it pays for itself.

The 183-day rule and when it kicks in

Portugal uses a 183-day rolling 12-month period to determine tax residency — not a fixed calendar year. This is important because it means even if you moved mid-year, you could trigger Portuguese residency faster than you think.

If you have been in Portugal for fewer than 183 days so far this year and your arrangement is genuinely temporary, your situation is simpler. You are likely still a UK tax resident and pay tax in the UK as normal.

Once you cross 183 days — or if your arrangement is clearly permanent — you are a Portuguese tax resident and the rules above apply.

Keep a precise record of the dates you are in each country. One day either side of a threshold can change your entire tax position for the year.

What your employer needs to know

This is the part most people ignore and it causes the most problems. Your employer has obligations too.

Once you are a permanent Portuguese resident, your UK employer may technically need to register for payroll in Portugal or use an Employer of Record service to pay you compliantly. Running PAYE on someone who is legally a Portuguese employee can create compliance risk for the employer — not just for you.

In practice, many smaller UK companies are slow to act on this. But it is something you should raise with your HR or payroll team. The conversation does not need to be dramatic — it is simply: I have moved permanently, and our payroll arrangement may need to be reviewed.

The NHR regime — does it help you?

Portugal used to offer a generous Non-Habitual Resident tax regime that gave new residents a flat 20% tax rate on Portuguese-source income for 10 years. The original NHR was closed to new applicants at the end of 2023.

A new version called IFICI — informally called NHR 2.0 — launched in 2024, but it is more targeted. It applies mainly to specific professions including technology, scientific research, and highly qualified employees in certain sectors. If your role qualifies, the benefit is significant. If not, you are taxed under standard Portuguese progressive rates which go up to 48% at the top end.

Check whether your role qualifies for IFICI before assuming you will pay standard Portuguese rates. A local tax advisor can confirm this in one conversation.

Pro tip

Even if you do not qualify for IFICI, Portugal is still generally tax-competitive compared to the UK for middle incomes. The standard Portuguese rates kick in lower, but the top rate only applies above around €80,000. Run the numbers for your specific income level before assuming you are worse off.

What to actually do next

Here is the practical sequence, in order:

  • Register as a tax resident in Portugal — get your NIF number if you do not have one, register at the local tax office (Finanças), and formally establish Portuguese residency.
  • Notify HMRC of your non-resident status — complete the P85 form to tell HMRC you have left the UK. This stops UK tax being over-withheld on your salary.
  • Tell your employer — they need to know your tax status has changed. Ask them to speak to their payroll provider about the implications.
  • Get a cross-border tax advisor for the first year — specifically someone who handles UK-Portugal situations. The first year is the most complicated because you are likely a tax resident of both countries for part of the year. Getting the split-year treatment right matters.
  • Check your IFICI eligibility — if your role is in technology or research, it is worth confirming whether you qualify for the favourable tax rate.
