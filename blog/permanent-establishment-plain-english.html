PE Risk

What actually happens if your remote worker stays too long in another country

Updated June 2026 · 7 min read · Informational only — not tax advice

Quick answer

If a remote employee spends too long in another country — or is in a senior or client-facing role there — the employer can be deemed to have a permanent establishment in that country. That means the employer may owe corporate income tax there, even with no office. This is not theoretical. Tax authorities in the UK, Germany, Australia and elsewhere have actively enforced this against companies with remote workers abroad.

Most employers who find themselves with a permanent establishment problem did not plan for it. They agreed to let someone work from abroad — temporarily, they thought — and months or years later discovered the arrangement had created a corporate tax liability in a foreign country.

Understanding how this happens is the first step to preventing it.

What permanent establishment actually means

A permanent establishment — PE — is a legal concept that says a company has a taxable business presence in a country even if it has no registered office there. Once a PE exists, that country's tax authority can require the company to file corporate tax returns and pay corporate income tax on profits attributable to activities in that country.

This is a company-level problem, not just an individual employee problem. The employee still has their own personal tax obligations — but the PE issue sits with the employer.

How a remote worker triggers it

There are two main ways a remote worker creates a PE for their employer.

Fixed place PE — the employee works from a fixed location in a foreign country on a regular, sustained basis. Their home office essentially becomes a place of business for the company. The more permanent and structured the arrangement, the higher the risk.

Agency PE — the employee has authority to sign contracts or make binding commitments on behalf of the company. This is the most serious trigger and does not require any time threshold. One employee with signing authority, working from home in a foreign country, can create an Agency PE immediately.

Important

Contract signing authority is an immediate PE trigger regardless of how many days the employee has been in the country. If your remote worker can legally commit the company to deals or agreements, the PE risk exists from day one.

What the OECD 2025 update changed

In November 2025 the OECD updated its Model Tax Convention guidance on remote work and PE. The key changes introduced two new tests that run together.

The 50% working time benchmark — if an employee spends more than 50% of their working time in a foreign country, the arrangement is presumed to have a sufficient degree of permanence to warrant PE assessment.

The commercial reason test — even above the 50% threshold, a PE is only created if the worker's presence in that country serves a genuine commercial purpose tied to the local market. A developer writing code from their Portuguese apartment is unlikely to meet this test. A sales executive regularly visiting local clients from the same apartment almost certainly does.

These two tests work together. Being above 50% time is not enough alone — the nature of the work matters enormously.

Why the worker's role matters more than their location

This is the part most articles miss. Two employees can be in the same country for the same number of days and have completely different PE risk profiles for their employer.

A software developer working remotely from Spain for a UK company, writing code with no local client contact, is genuinely low risk. Their work has no particular commercial connection to Spain — they could be anywhere.

A regional sales manager working from the same city in Spain, regularly meeting local clients, negotiating deals, representing the company commercially — that person's role has a direct commercial connection to Spain. The OECD commercial reason test is satisfied. PE risk is high regardless of whether they technically have signing authority.

Pro tip

When assessing your company's PE risk from a remote worker, ask one question: does this person's work generate value specifically because of where they are? If the answer is yes — because they serve local clients, speak the local language professionally, or represent the company in local markets — PE risk is significantly elevated.

What the company actually faces if PE is triggered

The consequences are serious and they are not theoretical.

  • Corporate income tax assessment — the foreign tax authority can assess corporate tax on profits it attributes to the PE. Working out how much profit to attribute to a home office PE is complex and contested, but the liability can be significant.
  • Penalties and interest — if the PE has existed for years without the company filing in that country, back taxes, penalties, and interest all accumulate.
  • Payroll registration requirements — once a PE is established, the company typically needs to register for local payroll and comply with local employment law.
  • Reputational risk — a PE enforcement action by a foreign tax authority is not the kind of thing investors, customers, or future employees look favourably on.

How to structure arrangements to reduce risk

The cleanest solutions in order of effectiveness:

  • Use an Employer of Record — an EOR legally employs the worker in their country on behalf of your company. This handles local payroll, compliance, and removes the PE risk from you. Services like Deel, Remote.com, and Rippling provide this.
  • Establish a local entity — if the arrangement is truly permanent and the market is important enough, set up a proper local subsidiary. This is the most compliant long-term structure.
  • Restrict signing authority — formally document that the worker has no authority to bind the company contractually. This removes the Agency PE risk, though it does not address Fixed Place PE for long-term arrangements.
  • Keep arrangements genuinely temporary — short-term remote work with a clear end date and total days below the 50% working time threshold is far lower risk than open-ended arrangements.
