PE Risk

Why your job title matters more than your location for cross-border tax

Updated June 2026 · 5 min read · Informational only — not tax advice

Quick answer

Two people can work from the same desk in the same country for the same employer and have completely different permanent establishment risk profiles — based purely on what their job involves. A developer writing code remotely is low risk. A sales executive meeting local clients is high risk. The OECD's commercial reason test is what separates them, and your job function determines whether you meet it.

Most people think about cross-border tax risk in terms of geography. How long have I been here? Which country am I in? What is the tax treaty between these two countries?

Geography matters. But for permanent establishment risk — the most serious corporate tax exposure in cross-border remote work — your job function often matters more than any of those things.

The question that determines everything

The OECD's 2025 updated guidance on remote work and permanent establishment introduced what it calls the commercial reason test. Stripped back to its simplest form, the test asks one question:

Does this employee's work generate value specifically because of where they are?

If yes — because they serve local clients, operate in local markets, speak to local customers professionally, or represent the company in that country's business environment — a PE is much more likely. The employee's presence in that country has a genuine commercial connection to that country's market.

If no — because they write code, manage data, create content, or provide technical support that could happen from anywhere — the commercial reason test is much harder for a tax authority to satisfy. Their presence in that country is incidental to their work, not integral to it.

What the commercial reason test actually is

Before the 2025 OECD update, the main test for PE was essentially: is the arrangement permanent enough, and does the employee spend enough time there? The 50% working time benchmark was introduced as a quantitative guide.

But the commercial reason test added a qualitative layer. Being above the time threshold is not enough to trigger PE on its own. The nature of the work — whether it has a genuine commercial connection to the worker's location — now forms part of the assessment.

This is good news for companies with technical or support staff working remotely from other countries. It is less good news for companies with sales, commercial, or executive staff doing so.

Risk levels by job function

Lower PE risk

Software developer, data analyst, content creator, graphic designer, customer support agent, accountant, HR specialist, project coordinator

Moderate PE risk

Marketing manager, product manager, business analyst, regional manager with no client contact, finance director with internal focus

Higher PE risk

Sales executive, account manager, country manager, business development director, anyone with signing authority, executive with local client relationships

These are general guides not absolute rules. The specific facts of each arrangement always matter. A marketing manager running global campaigns from their home country is different from one who is the company's face in a local market attending industry events and building local relationships.

Two workers, same location, different risk

Consider two employees of a UK company. Both live permanently in Spain. Both work from home. Both have been there for two years.

Employee A is a backend developer. They write code, attend remote standups, and have no contact with Spanish clients or the Spanish market. Their work is identical whether they are in Spain, Portugal, or anywhere else with good internet. They could be replaced by someone in a different country tomorrow without any impact on the company's Spanish commercial activities — because there are none.

Employee B is a Southern Europe sales manager. They hold meetings with Spanish clients, attend Spanish industry events, represent the company in the Spanish market, and their relationships with Spanish businesses are a direct commercial asset for the company. Their presence in Spain is not incidental — it is the point.

For Employee A, the commercial reason test likely fails. Low PE risk. For Employee B, it likely succeeds. High PE risk. Same country, same employer, same number of days, completely different exposure.

Pro tip

When assessing PE risk for a remote worker, ask whether their role would still make sense if they moved to a different country tomorrow. For a developer or analyst, the answer is almost always yes. For a sales or country manager serving a specific local market, the answer is almost always no. That question is essentially what the commercial reason test is asking.

Why signing authority overrides everything

There is one factor that bypasses the commercial reason test entirely: contract signing authority.

If an employee working abroad has the legal authority to sign contracts, close deals, or make binding commitments on behalf of the company — this creates what is called an Agency PE. It does not matter what their job title is. It does not matter how many days they have been there. It does not matter whether their role otherwise passes the commercial reason test.

Agency PE exists from the moment the arrangement begins, for any employee with signing authority in a foreign country.

The practical implication: if you have senior staff working abroad who have been granted signing authority as part of their role, this needs to be addressed immediately. Either the authority needs to be formally restricted and documented, or the company needs to establish proper local compliance structures.
